Marine Biology & Ecosystems

Marine biology is the study of organisms in the ocean. Since the ocean covers 71% of Earth, it provides a vast array of habitats, from the sunlit surface to the crushing pressure of the Hadal zone (trenches).

Scientists categorize marine life into three main groups: Plankton (drifters), Nekton (swimmers like fish and whales), and Benthos (bottom-dwellers like crabs and coral).

Physical Oceanography: Circulation

Ocean currents act as the Earth's heating and cooling system. Surface currents are driven by winds, while deep-ocean currents are driven by Thermohaline Circulation (density changes caused by salt and temperature).

This "Global Conveyor Belt" takes nearly 1,000 years to complete one full cycle, transporting nutrients and oxygen to the deep sea where life would otherwise struggle to survive.

Marine Geology: The Hidden Earth

The ocean floor is not flat; it features the largest mountain ranges on Earth (Mid-Ocean Ridges) and canyons deeper than the Himalayas are tall.

Plate Tectonics constantly reshape the seafloor. Hydrothermal vents on the ocean floor release mineral-rich water, supporting unique ecosystems that survive on chemical energy (chemosynthesis) instead of sunlight.

Chemical Oceanography: Acidification

As humans release more CO2, the ocean absorbs about 30% of it. This causes a chemical reaction that lowers the water's pH, known as Ocean Acidification.

This "osteoporosis of the sea" makes it difficult for calcifying organisms, like corals and oysters, to build their shells, threatening the entire marine food web.
